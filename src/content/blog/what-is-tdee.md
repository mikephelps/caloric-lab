If you've ever tried counting calories and wondered why the results didn't match expectations, there's a good chance the issue wasn't your willpower — it was your starting number.

**TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure)** is the total number of calories your body burns in a 24-hour period. It's the single most important number in any nutrition plan, and yet most people either don't know what it is or use a wildly inaccurate estimate.

## What TDEE Measures

Your TDEE accounts for everything your body burns in a day:

- **BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate):** The calories needed to keep your organs running at rest — breathing, circulation, cell repair, hormone production. This is the largest component, typically 60–75% of your TDEE.
- **TEF (Thermic Effect of Food):** Calories burned digesting and absorbing food — about 10% of total intake.
- **NEAT (Non-Exercise Activity Thermogenesis):** All movement that isn't structured exercise — walking to your car, fidgeting, cleaning the house.
- **EAT (Exercise Activity Thermogenesis):** Actual gym sessions, runs, or other deliberate exercise.

Add these up and you get TDEE.

## How TDEE Is Calculated

Most TDEE calculators — including CaloricLab — start with the **Mifflin-St Jeor equation**, which calculates your BMR from your age, height, weight, and sex. This equation is consistently ranked as the most accurate for the general population in peer-reviewed research.

That BMR is then multiplied by an **activity factor** ranging from 1.2 (sedentary) to 1.9 (extremely active), giving you your estimated TDEE.

## Why TDEE Matters for Your Goals

Think of TDEE as your maintenance level — the calorie intake at which your weight stays stable over time.

- **Eat below TDEE** → calorie deficit → fat loss
- **Eat at TDEE** → maintenance → weight stable
- **Eat above TDEE** → calorie surplus → weight gain (ideally muscle with proper training)

Once you know your TDEE, you can set an intelligent calorie target instead of guessing.

## The Biggest Mistake People Make

The #1 mistake: overestimating activity level. "Moderately active" sounds reasonable, but if you're doing 3 gym sessions a week and sitting at a desk the rest of the time, you're probably closer to "lightly active" — and the difference is 200+ calories per day.

That gap alone can explain why someone eating a "correct" number of calories isn't losing weight.
